## Reflection on smart questions
In school we are taught that there are no stupid questions. This principle is introduced to us to encourage people to talk instead of sitting silently while professors wait quietly after stimulating a clueless class with a question that no one was paying attention to. It's a way of keeping students interactive and engaged in a classroom setting, but what if someone asked something irrelevant to the nature of current material? What if you ask a question based off of matrial of a whole different subject? What if you asked a rudimentary question in a class that is specialized in building off of advanced technical material? Everyone would be able to hear and you'd be outted as the one person who speaks up in class and asks stupid questions.

Everything we've been taught up until now about asking questions had been misleading. In a technical field, asking the correct questions to get accurate and helpful information is important to growth in terms of skill. Asking dumb questions can actually be counterintuitive to learning and may send an individual on a wild goose chase, looking in all the wrong places for all the wrong information. Alongside wasting your own time on getting wrong information you're also wasting other people's time. It benefits the person helping if they were to just ignore the question and help someone else. In our field, this importance is going to come through asking questions on Stack Overflow.

## Imagine you're trying to answer the question
Stack Overflow is an important tool for computer engineers and software developers for the pure fact that other's who are experts in the field may be accessible to help with certain aspects of code and syntax. It is the largest, and most trusted online community for developers to learn, but in order to get help you must first ask smart questions.

"What constitutes a good question," one may ask. Well first and foremost, asking a smart question requires you to have done your utmost best to try to find the information yourself. Do some research on the topic. Is there any information online that may be resourceful or helpful to know? Have you read the manual or asked a skilled friend? If you're doing the most to find the answer yourself it shows that you're not just sitting around and waiting for the answer to appear in your hands. A smart question is also documented correctly. Making sure you're posting in the correct forum or subforum dedicated to the category of your question will allow the write readers to reach it. You're not going to get help on your machine learning neural network in a cooking forum (or maybe the chef has his masters in machine learning and you will, but that's beside the point).

An amazing example of a good question on Stack Overflow exists here: [How do I undero the most recent local commuits in Git](https://stackoverflow.com/questions/927358/how-do-i-undo-the-most-recent-local-commits-in-git)
Tagging git, version-control, git-commit, undo, and pre-commit, this person knew their audience. Looking in the right places for the right advice, this person recieved an abundance of help and the question was upvoted 20k times for being resourceful for the whole community. The question was simple and to the point and the person demonstrated that they had previous knowledge in Git.

An amazing example of a terrible question on Stack Overflow exists here: [Learning to write a compiler](https://stackoverflow.com/questions/1669/learning-to-write-a-compiler)
Being extremely vague, the answers ended up being all over the place with no real direction. Looking for resources for building a compiler, this person should have been a little more specific about what they were looking for and could have potentially shown where they have been looking.
