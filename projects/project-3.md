A flow art is a general term used to describe the intersection of a variety of movement-based disciplines including dance, juggling, fire-spinning, and object manipulation. Flow arts started as a drug related paraphernalia tied to the rave scene, but now there are communities thriving on flow arts as forms of performance and self representation. The art form community that I participate in and aspire to grow is the gloving community. LED gloves consist of typically white gloves with LEDs at the finger tips which create a variety of different trail patterns and color options. For the most part, people use programmable chips which allow you to swap out patterns and colors freely. 'Gloving is not a crime' is a movement that's trying to make that disconnect between the utilization of flow arts as a performing arts from drug related paraphernalia and is integral in creating a community that everyone can enjoy whether it be participating or even just spectating/supporting.

Having a local community and even a club at the University of Hawaii at Manoa, 'glovers' typically meet up and 'trade' shows. This process consists of two people who exchange light shows one at a time. As one person gloves the other sits back and enjoys. Some others may potentially oversee what's going on and spectate as well. Living in the internet age though, a large majority of people share their shows online in social media community groups or on YouTube. Many people make large productions of their videos and like to encorportate storytelling elements which attract viewers.

My involvement in the gloving scene is that I am a member of a team named Infinite Movement and president of the UH Manoa flow arts club. Infinite Movement is an all Hawaii team which is well regarded with a large number of supporters. Many of us participate in local and online tournaments which are based off of scorecards similar to those of dance competitions and score wise we have held the title as the number one team in the world (based off of points alone). Having a reputation world wide, we try our best to build and mentor our local community and share our practice and learning habits with others. More recently we ran a mentorship program where we took learning glovers and taught them our perspective of showmanship, presence and technique across the span of a year through weekly 'assignments' which them submitted as practice videos. Aside from that, our team is very close knit and keeping strong bonds between us allows us to push our creative boundaries.
